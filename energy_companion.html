🚢 The Strait of Hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow sea route between Iran and Oman, connecting the Persian Gulf to the open ocean. About 20% of the world's oil and a large share of LNG (liquefied natural gas) passes through it. If the strait is blocked or disrupted by conflict, global energy prices can rise sharply.

🏭 Asia as the World's Factory

Asia, especially East and Southeast Asia, manufactures a large share of the world's goods — from electronics and textiles to cars and chemicals. Since manufacturing requires a lot of energy, and most Asian countries import their energy, they are vulnerable to disruptions in global oil and gas supply chains.

⛽ The 1970s Oil Shocks

In 1973, some Arab oil-producing nations stopped exporting oil to nations that supported Israel during the Yom Kippur War. Oil prices rose sharply, causing economic problems like recession, inflation, and fuel shortages. Another shock followed in 1979 after the Iranian Revolution. These crises forced many countries to build strategic oil reserves and diversify their energy supplies.

💰 Fuel Subsidies & Government Balance Sheets

Many Asian governments keep fuel prices affordable by providing subsidies. This helps people but costs a lot of money. When global oil prices surge, maintaining subsidies can increase the budget deficit. Countries under IMF programmes (e.g., Pakistan) face additional pressure to cut subsidies, which directly hurts consumers.

The Economist · April 1st, 2026 · Geopolitics & Energy

How the Gulf's war is becoming Asia's crisis too

Prices, debt, and scarcity will strike a blow against the world's workshop

As missiles and drones fly across the Persian Gulf, their impact is being felt an ocean away. This war is "an Asian crisis", Vivian Balakrishnan, Singapore's foreign minister, told Reuters last week. Around 80% of the oil and 90% of the gas that usually pass through the Strait of Hormuz are bound for Asian markets.

Poor countries are being hit the hardest. In the Philippines more than 90% of energy imports come from the Middle East; Bangladesh, India and Pakistan receive almost two-thirds of their total LNG supplies via the strait. But rich Asian countries are far from immune. Japan boasts a strategic oil reserve equivalent to 254 days of domestic demand, a buffer built up following the oil shocks of the 1970s. But bus and ferry services across the country have been curtailed for lack of supply. Japanese bathhouses are struggling to make ends meet as fuel costs rise; several across the country have announced temporary or permanent closures. And Yamayoshi Seika, a popular snack-maker, had to temporarily stop production of potato crisps after running out of heavy oil for its fryers.

The strait's closure presents three big risks to Asian economies. The first is rising fuel prices. These will increase costs elsewhere and crimp growth, potentially causing a stagflationary spiral. The immediate pain is being felt by motorists across the region, but especially in South-East Asia. Globally, petrol prices have risen by 14% since the war began; in South-East Asian countries the figure is 42%. Prices in the Philippines and Myanmar have shot up by more than 70%, among the biggest jumps in the world.

Section 2 · Paragraphs 4–5 · Government Responses & Fiscal Risk

In other parts of Asia, such as India and Bangladesh, the jump has not yet been felt at the pumps — but that is only because their governments control fuel prices. On March 27th the Indian government said that it would slash central excise duties on petrol and diesel in order to keep pump prices from rising. Australia and Vietnam have promised similar measures to absorb higher oil prices. In South Korea, which imports 70% of its oil from the Middle East, the government has imposed a fuel-price cap to limit the damage.

The second risk, therefore, is to Asian governments' balance-sheets. Many already spend heavily to subsidise energy or set fuel prices, but the fiscal room available for such interventions varies enormously. In Indonesia rising fuel subsidies could result in the country breaching its fiscal-deficit cap of 3% of GDP. Investor confidence, already fragile, would erode further. Cash-strapped Pakistan, under IMF scrutiny, has already had to put up fuel prices by 20%. Such pressures could invite unwanted attention from speculators looking for shaky currencies. Japan's finance ministry is already said to be considering intervening in the oil-futures market to prop up the yen.

Section 3 · Paragraphs 6–7 · Inflation & Food Prices

Regardless of what governments do, some inflation is inevitable. Where governments do not absorb costlier crude, rising energy prices will fuel headline inflation. Crude-importing countries with weak exchange rates and big oil bills, such as the Philippines and Pakistan, will suffer the most. But even in those countries that apply fuel-price caps, the war in Iran will exert inflationary pressure through other channels. Supply-chain disruptions will push up costs in other industries, such as chemicals and logistics.

The biggest inflationary force could come through food. The war has ensnared roughly a third of global seaborne fertiliser trade, which will drive up food prices, especially when the sowing season begins later this year. The Asian Development Bank (ADB) had projected that prices in Asia would creep up by just 2.1% in 2026. It now warns that figure could exceed 5%, depending on how long the war lasts.

Section 6 · Language Focus — Hedging Language · 불확실성 표현
Why this matters: In journalism, not everything is a proven fact. Reporters use hedging expressions to signal when something is a prediction, an estimate, or an unconfirmed report. Recognising these signals helps you read critically and judge how confident the writer really is.
ExpressionSentence from ArticleWhat it signals
could"The energy shock could sap Asia's economic growth."Possibility — may or may not happen; writer does not commit.
may"Aviation and tourism may be the hardest hit."Soft prediction — avoids stating this as fact.
potentially"…potentially causing a stagflationary spiral."Worst-case scenario presented with caution; not a certainty.
suggests"Analysis from Kpler suggests the situation is precarious."Interpretation, not proof — weaker than "shows" or "proves".
estimates"Kpler estimates that the Philippines… hold just enough…"Approximate figure — not a confirmed measurement.
is said to be"Japan's finance ministry is said to be considering…"Unconfirmed / indirect source — writer cannot vouch for it.
warns"The ADB warns that figure could exceed 5%."Not hedging — signals urgency vs. neutral "says".
depending on"…could exceed 5%, depending on how long the war lasts."Attaches a condition — makes the claim explicitly contingent.

Compare the two sentences in each pair. Analyse the difference in meaning and effect on the reader.

Pair 1 — says vs warns
Sentence A"The ADB says prices could exceed 5%."
Sentence B (article)"The ADB warns prices could exceed 5%."
"Says"는 중립적으로 정보를 전달합니다. "Warns"는 긴박함과 경각심을 더해, ADB가 이 결과를 심각한 위협으로 본다는 뉘앙스를 줍니다. 동일한 사실도 어떤 동사를 선택하느냐에 따라 독자의 감정적 반응이 달라집니다.
Pair 2 — direct claim vs double hedging
Sentence A"The situation in the Philippines is precarious."
Sentence B (article)"Analysis from Kpler suggests the situation may be precarious."
문장 A는 상황을 확인된 사실로 단정합니다. 문장 B는 "suggests" + "may"로 이중 헤징합니다. 이렇게 하면 기자는 과장을 피하면서 독자가 스스로 판단하도록 유도할 수 있습니다. 이것이 신뢰할 수 있는 저널리즘의 특징입니다.
Pair 3 — direct knowledge vs indirect source
Sentence A"Japan is considering intervening in the oil-futures market."
Sentence B (article)"Japan's finance ministry is said to be considering intervening."
문장 A는 기자가 이 사실을 직접 확인한 것처럼 제시합니다. 문장 B의 "is said to be"는 미확인 또는 간접 출처에서 나온 정보임을 신호합니다. 독자는 이 표현을 보면 '소문이나 익명 출처의 정보'로 받아들이고, 더 신중하게 판단해야 한다는 신호를 받습니다.
